    <h2>Your Real Objective During the Stop</h2>
    <p>
      The officer on the roadside is performing a job: observing, asking questions, and deciding what to record. Many drivers try to argue their entire case at the window. That usually backfires. Your real objective is simpler: stay safe, stay professional, and avoid making statements that limit your options later.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>Pull over promptly and safely when it is clear the stop is directed at you.</li>
      <li>Turn off the radio, lower your window, and keep your hands visible on the wheel.</li>
      <li>Have license, registration, and insurance where you can access them without rummaging or sudden movements.</li>
      <li>Use a calm, neutral tone; you are not trying to “win” a debate on the shoulder.</li>

    </p>

    <ul>
      <li>“Yes, Officer.”</li>
      <li>“I understand.”</li>

    <h2>Officer Math Snapshot: Speed and Distance at Highway Speeds</h2>
    <p>
      Officers often combine judgment with measurement. Even without full formulas, it helps to understand the scale of what they are estimating.
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>At 65 miles per hour, a vehicle travels roughly 95 feet per second.</li>
      <li>At 75 miles per hour, the number is closer to 110 feet per second.</li>
      <li>At 80 miles per hour, it is about 117 feet per second.</li>
    </ul>
    <p>
      A brief two‑second observation window can represent more than 200 feet of travel. Curves, lane changes, and other vehicles can all affect how clear that observation really was.
